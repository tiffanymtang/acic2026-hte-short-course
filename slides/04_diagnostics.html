From Falco’s handoff

Falco closed with stability as a discovery criterion: which covariates plausibly modify the treatment effect?

The question now: once you’ve discovered subgroups, effect modifiers, or rules, should you trust them?

Possible concerns:

Three diagnostic questions

1. Goodness-of-fit

Is my model capturing any signal?

→ Omnibus test

2. Model selection

Among several candidate metalearners, which is best and how confident are we?

→ Relative error

3. Robustness

Are estimates sensitive to small changes in the dataset?

→ Influence points/sets

The shared obstacle: the individual treatment effect is never observed. Every diagnostic must be built around an observable proxy for fit.

Omnibus test for heterogeneity

Why standard quality checks fail

Typically, in supervised learning, we can rely on a variety of metrics, e.g., test error

For HTE estimation this no longer doesn’t applies:

  • Each unit reveals only \(Y_i(T_i)\), never both \(Y_i(1)\) and \(Y_i(0)\)
  • \(\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)\) is unobservable, so \((\hat\tau(X_i) - \tau_i)^2\) is uncomputable
  • Comparing \(\hat Y_i\) to \(Y_i\) doesn’t catch the relevant kind of misspecification. A metalearner can predict outcomes well and predict heterogeneity terribly.

We need a test that uses observable quantities but targets the unobservable \(\tau(X)\).

This is the motivation slide. The bullet about Y vs τ misspecification is worth dwelling on — it’s the most common confusion when people new to HTE see this material. They think “of course I can just compute MSE on Y” but that misses heterogeneity entirely.

The Best Linear Predictor (BLP) framing

Chernozhukov et al. (2018): project the true CATE onto \(\hat\tau(X)\) via the BLP: \[ \operatorname{BLP}\!\left[\tau(X)\mid \hat\tau(X)\right] \;=\; \alpha_\tau + \beta_\tau\bigl\{\hat\tau(X) - \mathbb{E}[\hat\tau(X)]\bigr\} \]

where \(\alpha_\tau = \mathbb{E}[\hat\tau(X)]\) and \(\beta_\tau = \dfrac{\operatorname{cov}(\tau(X),\,\hat\tau(X))}{\operatorname{var}(\hat\tau(X))}\).

Decomposing \(\beta_\tau\). Let \(v(X) = \tau(X) - \hat\tau(X)\). Then \[ \beta_\tau \;=\; 1 + \operatorname{cor}(v(X),\hat\tau(X))\cdot\frac{\operatorname{sd}(v(X))}{\operatorname{sd}(\hat\tau(X))} \]

\(\beta_\tau = 1\) means residual error is uncorrelated with the prediction — the metalearner has captured the relevant signal.

The BLP slide is dense — walk through slowly. Key intuition: β captures whether residual structure is correlated with the prediction. If you’re systematically wrong in a τ̂-correlated way, β ≠ 1. The decomposition makes the “uncorrelated residuals” intuition concrete.

What different values of \(\beta_\tau\) look like

Three regimes for β_τ. Left: well-fit (β≈1, residuals uncorrelated with τ̂). Center: under-prediction of variation (β>1, predictions too compressed). Right: no signal (β≈0, residuals are all the structure).

Dashed line: \(\beta = 1\) (perfect calibration). Orange: fitted slope of \(\tau\) on \(\hat\tau\).

Three intuition pictures. Important point: β > 1 is more common than people expect — happens when a regularized metalearner over-shrinks. β ≈ 0 is the “no heterogeneity captured” case. Audience should leave knowing β captures calibration of the heterogeneity, not just whether it exists.

Estimating \(\beta_\tau\) in practice

Chernozhukov et al. (2018) show \(\beta_\tau\) can be recovered from a Neyman-orthogonal regression:

\[ Y_i = \hat\gamma_0 + \hat\gamma_x \hat b_0(X_i) \;+\; \hat\alpha_\tau\{Z_i - \hat\pi(X_i)\} \;+\; {\color{#1f4e79}\hat\beta_\tau}\{Z_i - \hat\pi(X_i)\}\bigl\{\hat\tau(X_i) - \hat{\mathbb{E}}[\hat\tau(X_i)]\bigr\} + \hat\varepsilon_i \]

where \(\hat b_0\) is the control outcome model and \(\hat\pi\) is the propensity score.

Test: \(\;H_0: \beta_\tau = 0 \;\;\text{vs}\;\; H_A: \beta_\tau \neq 0\)

Reject \(\Rightarrow\) \(\hat\tau\) captures real heterogeneity.

Equivalence test: \(H_0:\beta_\tau\neq 1\) vs \(H_A:\beta_\tau = 1\)

What “fail to reject” can mean:

  • \(\tau(X)\) is genuinely constant (no heterogeneity to find), or
  • \(\hat\tau(X)\) is poorly estimated and the test simply has no power

Show the regression structure but don’t dwell on the derivation — point out that β̂_τ is the coefficient on the (Z-π̂)·(τ̂ - mean τ̂) interaction. The “what fail-to-reject means” panel is critical context: a non-significant test is ambiguous between “no effect” and “bad model.”

Cross-fitting matters here too

The orthogonal regression on the previous slide uses plug-in estimates of \(\hat\pi\), \(\hat b_0\), and \(\hat\tau\) — all functions of the same data.

Without sample splitting, asymptotic normality of \(\hat\beta_\tau\) fails: residuals from the auxiliary fits leak into the test. Standard errors become unreliable.

Recipe.

  • Split data into folds \(\mathcal{D}_1,\dots,\mathcal{D}_K\)
  • For each fold \(k\): fit nuisances \((\hat\pi, \hat b_0, \hat\tau)\) on \(\mathcal{D}_{-k}\)
  • Plug into the orthogonal regression and compute \(\hat\beta_\tau^{(k)}\) on \(\mathcal{D}_k\)
  • Aggregate \(\hat\beta_\tau\) as the (median or mean) across folds; standard error from across-fold variance plus within-fold variance

Yields the asymptotic normality you need for hypothesis testing.

What the test does, and doesn’t, tell you

When the omnibus test rejects:

  • You have evidence that \(\hat\tau\) captures some signal
  • The model is at least minimally calibrated to \(\tau\)
  • It does not tell you the model is right everywhere or that the discovered subgroups are correct

Relative error

IS THIS THE BEST AMONG ALTERNATIVES?

The model-selection problem

In practice you fit multiple metalearners — say a causal forest, an X-learner with random forest, an R-learner with LASSO — and they disagree.

Which one should you pick?

Without ground truth \(\tau_i\), you can’t compute the obvious metric (test MSE on \(\tau\)).

Goal: a procedure that

  • works on observed data only (no oracle \(\tau\))
  • yields a confidence set \(\hat S\) for the winning estimator
  • controls the probability that the true winner is missed at level \(\alpha\)

Note: “winner” here means lowest MSE on \(\tau\). We can’t compute that — but we can rank candidates relative to each other.

The Grant figure with five different metalearners on the same breast-cancer data is a perfect motivator here. Mention it verbally. The selection problem is “which of these to trust” with no oracle.

What about PEHE and policy risk?

Existing alternatives

  • PEHE [Hill 2011]: requires ground-truth \(\tau\) — only usable in semi-synthetic benchmarks
  • Policy risk [Shalit et al. 2017]: evaluates recommendation quality, not estimation quality; doesn’t recover the best \(\hat\tau\)
  • Influence-function approaches: compute risk gradients but don’t formalize the selection question

None of these gives you a confidence set for the winning estimator.

The relative-error approach

Instead of estimating \(\mathbb{E}[(\hat\tau - \tau)^2]\) directly (which requires \(\tau\)), estimate the difference: \[ \delta(\hat\tau_1, \hat\tau_2) \;=\; \mathbb{E}[(\hat\tau_1 - \tau)^2] - \mathbb{E}[(\hat\tau_2 - \tau)^2] \]

The unobservable \(\tau\) enters \(\delta\) only through one term. We can estimate it.

Compare-and-contrast with existing methods. Audience will know PEHE; they may not know the limitations. Frame the relative-error approach as “the right way to do model selection in HTE.”

Pairwise relative error (Gao, 2024)

For two estimators \(\hat\tau_1, \hat\tau_2\), the difference in MSE is \[ \delta(\hat\tau_1, \hat\tau_2) \;\triangleq\; \mathbb{E}[(\hat\tau_1(X) - \tau(X))^2] - \mathbb{E}[(\hat\tau_2(X) - \tau(X))^2] \]

Expanding the squares: \[ \delta(\hat\tau_1, \hat\tau_2) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[\hat\tau_1^2(X) - \hat\tau_2^2(X)\right] - 2\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[(\hat\tau_1(X) - \hat\tau_2(X))\,\tau(X)\right] \]

Why this matters. The dependence on the unobserved \(\tau\) is now first-order: \(\tau(X)\) enters \(\delta\) as an inner product against \((\hat\tau_1 - \hat\tau_2)\), not as a squared term.

→ Plug-in estimation of \(\tau\) contributes only first-order error to \(\hat\delta\).

The “trick” slide. The cancellation that turns squared-error differences into a first-order target is the key insight. It’s why we can do ground-truth-free inference at all. Spend time here — the audience needs to feel the payoff.

A one-step estimator with cross-fitting

Plug in nuisance estimates \(\tilde\mu_1, \tilde\mu_0, \tilde e\) via a Neyman-orthogonal one-step: \[ \hat\delta(\hat\tau_1,\hat\tau_2) = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat t(Z_i;\hat\tau_1,\hat\tau_2) \]

where \(\hat t\) is an augmented IPW–style influence function for \(\delta\).

Use \(K\)-fold cross-fitting: train \(\tilde\mu_1, \tilde\mu_0, \tilde e\) on \(\mathcal{D}_{-k}\), evaluate \(\hat\delta\) on \(\mathcal{D}_k\), average.

Asymptotic normality (Gao, 2024). Under standard overlap, consistency, and stability of the nuisance estimators, \[ \frac{\hat\delta - \delta}{\sqrt{\widehat V/n}} \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \mathcal{N}(0,1). \]

This is the statistical foundation that lets us turn pairwise comparisons into hypothesis tests.

Don’t dwell on the specific form of t̂. Important point: cross-fitting + one-step gives asymptotic normality. That’s what we’ll lean on for testing.

Argmin inference: framing the selection problem

For \(K\) candidate estimators, we want a confidence set \(\hat S\) for the winner \(\hat\tau_{(1)}\).

For each candidate \(r \in \{1,\dots,K\}\), test whether \(r\) dominates all competitors: \[ H_0^{(r)}:\;\delta(\hat\tau_r,\hat\tau_s) < 0\;\;\forall s\neq r \quad\text{vs}\quad H_A^{(r)}:\;\exists s:\delta(\hat\tau_r,\hat\tau_s) > 0 \]

Family-wise error rate (FWER) control. \(\hat S\) contains the true winner with probability \(\geq 1 - \alpha\): \[ \liminf_{n\to\infty} \mathbb{P}(\hat\tau_{(1)} \in \hat S) \geq 1 - \alpha. \]

Any candidate failing to reject \(H_0^{(r)}\) is kept in \(\hat S\); we only exclude estimators we have strong evidence are not the winner.

Argmin inference framing — note the connection to ranking inference Tiffany or Falco may have mentioned. We’re inverting a hypothesis test to get a confidence set, in the spirit of Hodges & Lehmann.

Naive baseline: a max-statistic test

For each candidate \(r\), build pairwise \(z\)-statistics and test against the worst one: \[ S_{r,s} \;=\; \frac{\hat\delta(\hat\tau_r,\hat\tau_s)}{\hat\sigma_{r,s}}, \qquad S_r^{\max} = \max_{s\neq r} S_{r,s}. \]

Add \(r\) to \(\hat S\) if \(S_r^{\max}\) is below its \(1-\alpha\) null quantile (estimated by Gaussian resampling using \(\hat\Sigma_r\)).

The problem. The max-statistic treats every competitor symmetrically. As \(K\) grows:

  • A clearly-inferior estimator \(\hat\tau_s\) contributes a \(z\)-statistic that’s not informative about whether \(r\) is the winner — but inflates the critical value
  • Power against close competitors collapses
  • Effect: fewer rejections → larger \(\hat S\) → worse selection

The “why naive doesn’t work” slide. Intuition: max-statistic methods penalize you for adding obviously-bad candidates. We want a method that down-weights obvious losers automatically.

A more powerful test: exponentially weighted aggregation

For estimator \(\hat\tau_r\), build a weighted aggregate of pairwise relative errors per data point: \[ Q_{i,r} \;=\; \sum_{j\neq r} \hat\omega_{r,j}\, \hat t(Z_i;\hat\tau_r,\hat\tau_j), \qquad \hat\omega_{r,j} \propto \exp\bigl(\eta\,\hat\delta(\hat\tau_r,\hat\tau_j)\bigr) \]

Idea. Up-weight competitors that look genuinely close to \(r\); down-weight obviously-inferior competitors that would otherwise inflate the critical threshold.

The exponential mechanism (from differential privacy literature) provides the right rate of weight decay.

Result: powerful argmin inference that adapts to which competitors are close to the winner, not how many estimators are in the candidate set.

Exponential weighting is the headline. Tunable parameter η controls how aggressively to discount. In the paper this is set via cross-validation.

Why naive weighting doesn’t work — and the fix

Why we can’t just plug \(\hat\delta\) into weights and use the same fold:

The pairwise statistics \(\hat t(Z_i;\hat\tau_r,\hat\tau_j)\) across \(j\) are correlated (they share the same \(Z_i\)). Cross-fold weighting leaks information into the test, breaking asymptotic normality of \(Q_{i,r}\) and inflating FWER.

Two-layer cross-fitting structure.

  • Outer split (\(\mathcal{D} \to A,B\)): nuisance fit on \(A\), evaluate \(\hat\delta\) on \(B\) — and vice versa
  • Inner split within each outer fold: weight learning on one half, test statistic computation on the other

→ Weights and test statistics are computed on independent samples, restoring asymptotic normality of \(Q_{i,r}\).

The technical-hygiene slide. Key insight: you have to separate weight learning from testing — can’t reuse the same data, even with K-fold cross-validation, because of within-Z_i correlation across pairwise t-statistics. The two-layer design is the technical contribution.

Empirical: false-selection rate vs \(K\)

Sample finding. On benchmark datasets (ACIC, IHDP, Twins), as \(K\) grows the naive method’s false-selection rate inflates. The weighted method remains stable.

Practical takeaway. Don’t hand-pick a single metalearner. Run a few candidates, compute pairwise relative errors with cross-fitting, and report a confidence set for the winner.

Practical workflow

Step-by-step recipe.

  • Choose a small set of candidate metalearners — say 3–5 — covering different bias-variance tradeoffs
  • Run the omnibus test on each (Part 1)
  • For candidates that pass: compute all pairwise \(\hat\delta(\hat\tau_r, \hat\tau_s)\) with proper cross-fitting
  • Apply the weighted argmin test to construct \(\hat S\)
  • Report \(\hat S\) and visualize \(\hat\delta\) across pairs — even when there’s a clear winner, the magnitudes are informative

Don’t: pick a single estimator before running diagnostics, hoping it works.

Do: treat model selection as an inferential problem with calibrated uncertainty.

Practical-takeaway slide. Audience members who want to walk away with concrete advice get it here. The implicit message: this is a much more honest workflow than “I tried five learners and used the one that looked good.”

Influence points

Are results sensitive to small changes in the dataset?

Why we need influence diagnostics

Both omnibus tests and pairwise relative-error comparisons can be driven by a small handful of points.

The classical answer (regression). Cook’s distance: leave each point out, see how the fit moves. Robust statistics has been doing this for 40 years.

The HTE problem. \(\tau_i\) is unobservable, so we can’t measure “fit” the usual way — and dropping one point at a time misses the structure of how points interact.

Goal. Find the set of training points whose removal would most change downstream CATE predictions — and use that set as a stability summary.

Frame this as the “meta-diagnostic.” Cook’s distance for HTE — but generalized to sets, not single points, and to predictions on a held-out test set, not training residuals.

Outliers in \(\tau\) are invisible in \(Y\)

A clean Y(1)/Y(0) view hides 10 outliers in τ. We need diagnostics that target the unobservable τ surface, not the observable Y.

Defining the influence set

Let \(v\in\{0,1\}^{n_{\text{tr}}}\) index which training points we keep (\(v_i=1\)) or drop (\(v_i=0\)).

Let \(\hat g(v) = g_\tau(X_{\text{test}};\hat\theta(v))\) be the predicted CATEs on the test set when refit using only \(v_i = 1\) training points.

Prediction shift. \[ \phi(v) \;=\; \|\hat g(v) - \hat g(\mathbf{1})\|_2^2. \]

By construction \(\phi(\mathbf{1}) = 0\). Large \(\phi(v)\) ⇒ removing this set substantially changes the CATE predictions.

Three crucial design choices:

  • Evaluate on a held-out test set (not training residuals)
  • Look at sets, not single points
  • Target prediction shifts in \(\hat\tau\), not changes in \(Y\) predictions

Three things to land. Walk through each. The “evaluate on held-out test set” choice is what distinguishes our setup from training-residual approaches like Cook’s.

The optimization problem

For a target removal proportion \(\rho\): \[ \arg\max_{v\in\{0,1\}^{n_{\text{tr}}}} \phi(v) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \|v\|_0 = (1-\rho)\,n_{\text{tr}}. \]

An adversarial / worst-case characterization of training-set sensitivity: which \(\rho n_{\text{tr}}\) points, if removed, would most change downstream predictions?

Why this is hard.

  • \(\binom{n_{\text{tr}}}{\rho n_{\text{tr}}}\) candidate sets — combinatorially intractable for moderate \(n\)
  • Each evaluation of \(\phi(v)\) requires refitting the full metalearner pipeline → very expensive
  • The metalearner is a multi-stage estimation pipeline; the influence of removing \(i\) propagates through nuisance estimates and the CATE step

Plan: linearize the dependence of \(\hat g\) on the dropped points using influence functions, recover a tractable optimization.

This slide motivates everything that follows. Audience should leave with: combinatorial → linearize → solve a tractable surrogate. We’re not solving the exact problem; we’re solving an accurate approximation.

Multi-stage estimation: influence chains through \(\theta\)

A metalearner has multiple estimated components: \[ \theta = (\hat\pi(\cdot),\, \hat m_0(\cdot),\, \hat m_1(\cdot)), \qquad g_\tau(X_{\text{test}};\theta) = \text{CATE predictions}. \]

Removing a single point \(i\) changes each component. The downstream effect on \(\hat g\) is the chain of all these changes.

First-order Taylor expansion. \[ \frac{\partial \hat g(v)}{\partial v_i}\bigg|_{v=\mathbf{1}} \;=\;\underbrace{\frac{\partial g_\tau(X_{\text{test}};\theta)}{\partial \theta}\bigg|_{\hat\theta(\mathbf{1})}}_{J_g\,:\, \text{multi-stage Jacobian}} \;\times\;\underbrace{\frac{\partial \hat\theta(v)}{\partial v_i}\bigg|_{\mathbf{1}}}_{\text{IF}_\theta(i)\,:\,\text{classical IF}} \]

\(J_g\) encodes how predicted CATEs respond to changes in nuisance components — across propensity, outcome models, and the \(\tau\)-estimation step.

Technically dense — make sure to land that θ has multiple components and the chain rule passes through all of them. This is the part that’s new relative to standard influence-function approaches: those handle a single estimator; we handle the full pipeline.

Single-point influence — closed form

Lemma (single-point IF). Dropping observation \(i\) shifts predictions by approximately \[ \text{IF}_g(i) \;=\; -\,J_g\, H_\theta^{-1}\, \tfrac{1}{n_{\text{tr}}} \nabla_\theta \ell(\hat\theta(\mathbf{1});X_i,Z_i,Y_i) \] where \(H_\theta = \tfrac{1}{n_{\text{tr}}}\sum_k \nabla_\theta^2 \ell(\hat\theta(\mathbf{1});X_k,Z_k,Y_k)\) is the training Hessian.

For a single dropped point: \[ \phi(v^{(-i)}) \;\approx\; \|\text{IF}_g(i)\|_2^2. \]

Connection to Cook’s distance. This is a generalized Cook’s distance — the inverse Hessian + score gradient structure is the same; the new ingredient is the multi-stage Jacobian \(J_g\) that propagates the influence through to predictions on \(X_{\text{test}}\).

Audience members who’ve taught regression diagnostics will see Cook’s distance immediately. Mention this — familiar anchor. The novelty is the metalearner-aware J_g.

Estimating \(Q\) via auto-differentiation

The pairwise quadratic form \(Q_{ij} = 2\,\text{IF}_g(i)^\top \text{IF}_g(j)\) requires:

  • \(J_g\) — Jacobian of metalearner output w.r.t. \(\theta\)
  • \(H_\theta^{-1}\) — inverse Hessian of training loss
  • \(\nabla_\theta \ell\) — gradient at each training point

Computing \(H_\theta^{-1}\) is the bottleneck. For metalearners with thousands of parameters, forming \(H_\theta\) explicitly is infeasible.

Solution. Use forward-mode autodiff to compute Hessian-vector products \(H_\theta v\) on demand; solve \(H_\theta x = b\) via Conjugate Gradient Descent (CGD).

Practical points.

  • Each CGD step costs one HVP evaluation
  • Convergence in ~50–200 iterations for typical problems
  • Memory: \(O(p_\theta)\) rather than \(O(p_\theta^2)\)
  • Works for any differentiable metalearner: ridge R-learner, neural-network learners, etc.

Computational practicalities. Autodiff + CGD is the standard recipe — borrowed from optimization-of-bilevel-problems literature. Anyone who’s implemented an influence function for a deep network has seen this. Mention briefly that this is what makes the method scalable.

What if the metalearner isn’t smooth?

Causal forests, boosted trees, kernel learners — these aren’t differentiable in \(\theta\). The chain-rule derivation breaks.

Fallback. Replace analytic IF with finite-difference influence: \[ \text{IF}_g(i) \;\approx\; \hat g(\mathbf{1} - e_i) - \hat g(\mathbf{1}) \]

Compute by actually refitting with point \(i\) dropped. Costs \(n_{\text{tr}}\) refits up front, but cached for the optimization.

Hybrid practice. Use analytic IF where possible (smooth components like ridge); finite-difference for non-smooth components. The pairwise structure still admits a BQP formulation; the only thing that changes is how \(Q\) is estimated.

Ridge R-learner has analytic forms; causal forest needs finite-difference.

Important caveat. Audience members who plan to use causal forests need to know this. Good news: the rest of the framework (BQP, influence sets, breakdown points) doesn’t change — only the estimation of Q.

Why ranking by IF fails: redundancy and cancellation

The natural temptation: rank training points by \(\|\text{IF}_g(i)\|^2\), drop the top \(k\).

Why it fails.

  • Two high-IF points may exert redundant influence — dropping both gives roughly the same shift as dropping one
  • Two moderate-IF points may cancel individually but exert large joint influence when dropped together

The full quadratic form has cross terms: \[ \phi(v) \;\approx\; (v-\mathbf{1})^\top Q\,(v-\mathbf{1}) \] \[ Q_{ij} = 2\,\text{IF}_g(i)^\top \text{IF}_g(j) \]

Top-\(k\) ranking ignores the off-diagonal \(Q_{ij}\) — exactly the term that captures redundancy and cancellation.

Existing methods (Broderick et al. 2020, Koh & Liang 2017) rank by diagonal alone and miss this structure.

The “why naive doesn’t work” slide. The Q decomposition is a clean way to show off-diagonals matter. Tie back to existing literature: Broderick et al. (2020), Koh & Liang (2017) are top-k methods. Our contribution is to handle the cross terms.

BQP1: a tractable optimization

Substituting the quadratic form into the influence-set objective gives a Binary Quadratic Program: \[ \max_{v\in\{0,1\}^{n_{\text{tr}}}} \;(v-\mathbf{1})^\top Q\,(v-\mathbf{1}) \quad\text{s.t.}\quad \|v\|_0 = (1-\rho)\,n_{\text{tr}}. \]

Why this is good.

  • Off-the-shelf MIQP solvers (Gurobi, CPLEX) give global optimality guarantees
  • Single-point case is recovered as a special case
  • Approximation error is \(O(\rho^2 n_{\text{tr}}^2)\) from the truncated Taylor series

We call this the first-order BQP (BQP1).

The “binary quadratic program” framing is what makes this practical — solvers are mature. Briefly mention global optimality is meaningful here because greedy / top-k are local methods.

BQP2: second-order correction for joint deletions

For more accurate joint-deletion behavior, expand to second order. The quadratic form becomes \[ \phi(v) \;\approx\; (v-\mathbf{1})^\top Q^{(2)}\,(v-\mathbf{1}), \qquad Q^{(2)} = Q^{(1)} + R \]

\(R\) is a tensor-decomposition correction that captures second-order changes in the metalearner refit when pairs of points are dropped together.

Why this matters. When points are correlated in the underlying \(X\) space, removing both of them induces interactions that first-order misses. \(R\) recovers them.

Approximation error: \(O(\rho^3 n_{\text{tr}}^3)\) — qualitatively better than BQP1 in regimes where pairwise interactions matter.

The R correction is the technical novelty. Detail level: mention it’s a tensor decomposition computed via automatic differentiation but don’t derive it. Audience just needs to know there’s a principled higher-order term.

Method hierarchy

Three tiers

Method Single-point Joint deletions Solver complexity
Naive top-\(k\) exact ignores correlations trivial
BQP1 exact first-order MIQP
BQP2 exact second-order MIQP, larger \(Q\)

Each row strictly contains the previous — naive ⊂ BQP1 ⊂ BQP2 in expressive power.

Why all three? BQP1 is fast and good when points are nearly independent; BQP2 is necessary when correlations matter (which is most of the time). Naive is included for comparison and for situations where MIQP isn’t available.

  • Greedy/top-\(k\) for sets

Used for outlier detection, training-data attribution.

Our contribution

  • Multi-stage metalearners (CATE setting)
  • Adversarial set-level influence
  • Full \(Q\) with off-diagonals + tensor correction \(R\)
  • Global-optimum sets via MIQP

Used for HTE diagnostic, latent-subgroup discovery, breakdown-point estimation.

Reduction. When the metalearner is single-stage and the off-diagonals are zero, our framework reduces to Koh-Liang/Broderick. We strictly generalize.

JTPA application: the prediction-shift result

Setup. Job Training Partnership Act data. Four sites: Cedar Rapids IA, Corpus Christi TX, Larimer County CO, Providence RI.

Two metalearners (Ridge R-Learner, Causal Forest), tested influence sets at \(\rho \in \{1\%, 5\%, 10\%, 20\%\}\).

Result. BQP2 finds influence sets that produce substantially larger downstream prediction shifts than BQP1, naive top-\(k\), or random — consistently across all sites and learners.

Larger shift means the method is identifying genuinely high-leverage training points, not just noise.

The four-sites × two-learners figure is the empirical headline. BQP2 dominates across sites and learners — the kind of robustness audiences want to see.

JTPA application: the latent subgroup

The most striking finding wasn’t how much the predictions shifted — it was whose predictions shifted.

Influence set as latent subgroup. The 10% influence set identified by BQP2 corresponds primarily to individuals with high previous earnings — a coherent subgroup that BQP1 and naive miss.

Read the figure (right): each panel shows test CATEs from the full-data fit (x-axis) vs after dropping the influence set (y-axis), colored by previous earnings. Dashed line: no change.

BQP2 reveals an orange/red cluster (high earners) that pulls dramatically off the diagonal — that’s the subgroup being uncovered.

This is the slide that should land hardest. The influence set isn’t just a stability summary — it’s a discovery tool for subgroups where the metalearner is unstable. Dovetails directly back to Falco’s discovery framing: we’ve come full circle.

Breakdown point: a single-number summary

Definition (breakdown point for HTE). Given a target prediction-shift threshold \(\phi^*\), define \[ \rho_{\text{break}} \;=\; \min_{\rho}\Bigl\{\rho \,:\; \max_v \phi(v) > \phi^*,\;\;\|v\|_0 = (1-\rho)n_{\text{tr}}\Bigr\}. \]

The smallest fraction of training data we’d need to drop to induce a \(\phi^*\) shift in CATE predictions.

Why this is useful.

  • Single-number summary of model robustness
  • Comparable across metalearners on the same data
  • Connects to robust-statistics breakdown point (Hampel, Huber)
  • Directly actionable: “if I lose more than \(\rho_{\text{break}} \cdot n\) patients to dropout, my conclusions are at risk”

Practical use. Compare two candidate metalearners; pick the one with the larger \(\rho_{\text{break}}\) when the omnibus and relative-error tests are inconclusive.

Single-number summary is what gets used in tables and grant reports. The connection to robust statistics breakdown point (Hampel) is worth flagging — same conceptual object, different operationalization.

How the three diagnostics connect

Vertical structure.

  • Omnibus → “is there signal?”
  • Relative error → “is this signal the best?”
  • Influence functions → “is the answer to (1) and (2) robust?”

Run them in this order: omnibus first to filter candidates, relative error to rank, influence functions to validate the conclusion.

Cross-connections.

  • Influence functions can target the omnibus statistic itself, not just CATE predictions — find the points that drive a rejection (Part 3 forward-look)
  • Relative-error comparisons can be perturbed by influence sets — check whether the winner \(\hat S\) is stable to dropping a small fraction of data
  • The robust LTS estimator in the omnibus test is itself an influence-function-style downweighting

The three diagnostics aren’t a checklist — they’re a single connected framework for using observable structure to validate models of an unobservable target.

Synthesis slide. The cross-connections are what make this a coherent framework rather than three separate methods. The LTS-as-influence-downweighting connection is genuine and worth flagging — it ties Part 1 to Part 3 conceptually.
